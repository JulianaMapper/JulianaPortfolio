PH360: Built for the people running local public health

AI platform for departments that can't afford enterprise tools — HIPAA-compliant, Sunshine Law-ready, built on AWS Bedrock.

The situation

Local public health departments are chronically under-resourced. They're handling disease surveillance, environmental health complaints, emergency response, and a hundred other functions — often with teams of five to twenty people. After COVID, many were asked to do even more with even less.

I'd been working in and around local health departments for years. I knew the workflows. I knew the pain. When I saw Claude's capabilities demonstrated at an AWS event, I didn't think "enterprise use case." I thought about the environmental health specialist in a small county spending half her day answering the same food code questions by phone.

The challenge wasn't technical appetite. It was that the tools that existed weren't built for public health's unique constraints: HIPAA compliance, Sunshine Law data retention requirements, limited IT staff, and leadership that needed policy cover before they could say yes to anything new.

What I heard

I personally found people, structured the interviews, and led 50+ conversations with public health professionals across the US. The real blocker wasn't technology — it was policy ambiguity. Agencies didn't know what was allowed. "You can't use Google Docs but you can use a commercial AI tool" — the rules were inconsistent and nobody could explain them.

The second finding: even where AI was technically permitted, staff had no path to building a compliant knowledge base. Their information was locked in forms, case reports, food code manuals, and legacy systems — with no clear way to make it searchable without creating legal exposure.

The interviews also surfaced a product opportunity I hadn't planned for: organizations didn't just need a tool — they needed help writing the AI policy that would let them use it. That became a separate offering.

How I built it

The first prototype was entirely hand-coded by me — before AI coding tools existed. We had grant funding for a specific project and I started with the simplest thing that could work: a chat interface for asking questions of a food code document. I ran structured comparative tests across 5+ LLMs using a script I built to swap API keys — testing on real tasks, not benchmarks — before committing to AWS Bedrock. We tested different embeddings models and chunking strategies for the RAG knowledge base. Then iterated from there: food code chat → photo scanning for restaurant inspections → full knowledge base management.

What I built
HIPAA-compliant AI chatbot builder on AWS Bedrock with RAG and document upload
Sunshine Law-compliant data retention — purpose-built for public records requirements
Modular Salesforce platform with insurance reimbursement capabilities
AI Policy Guide: "12 Things to Include in an AI Policy for Public Health Departments" — free, adopted nationally
AI Policy Workshop offering — delivered first with Public Health Services Council of Ohio, built directly from what discovery interviews revealed
Monthly AI for Public Health Community of Practice — peer learning with guest speakers
Process mapping methodology: mapped Sauk County's workflows using the Head/Heart filter before touching any technology
What changed

Tasks that used to take 30 minutes now take 5. Sauk County staff can get an answer on a routine question in the time it takes to make a cup of coffee — instead of digging through binders or calling the state. That's the 83% reduction in real terms.

A small initial engagement grew into a flagship partnership. The AI Policy Guide became the resource health departments use to get leadership buy-in before they can say yes to anything new.

The situation

The U.S. military needed a unified tool for evaluating base locations worldwide — and had been making those decisions with fragmented data and gut instinct. There was no playbook. Nobody had built this before.

My mentor Jim Westervelt — creator of GRASS GIS — gave me the only advice that mattered on day one: go talk to the people who will use it before you build anything. I've done that ever since.

What I heard

I conducted user engagement across DOD to understand what data they trusted, what decisions they actually needed to make, and what "good enough" looked like in the field versus in a briefing room. The answer was different in every theater.

The presenting need was "a better map." The real need was a decision support system that could integrate dozens of conflicting data sources and give commanders something they could trust under pressure.

What I built
Unified analytical back-end integrating diverse DOD and open-source data sources
Data governance framework — review process, governance board, staff education from scratch
UX/UI collaboration on wireframes and interface design with internal team
ENSITE HUB for field data collection
Multiple ERDC/CERL technical reports and a peer-reviewed journal article
Puerto Rico — Hurricane Maria Response

Deployed as GIS Team Lead to Puerto Rico after Hurricane Maria. Built the geospatial platform supporting the Army Corps power restoration mission — reduced daily data updates from 8 hours to 30 minutes. Six months embedded with emergency response teams, coordinating geospatial data across federal and local agencies under active disaster conditions.

What changed

ENSITE deployed to Iraq, Afghanistan, and Poland after Russia's invasion of Ukraine — reducing the time to locate and evaluate potential military base sites. I received the Department of the Army Medal for Civilian Service for the CB-SITE leadership component.

The data governance framework I built from scratch became the standard for how CERL managed geospatial data products.

The situation

I inherited a geospatial demand forecasting model built by outside consultants. Variables were hard-coded. File paths were hard-coded. And — the thing that stopped me cold — nobody had ever validated it.

The company was making multi-year, multi-million dollar e-commerce facility network decisions — where to build warehouses, which stores to use for fulfillment — on a model nobody had stress-tested. I couldn't leave that alone.

What I heard

Before touching anything, I conducted user assessments and led focus groups to understand how the model was actually being used versus how it was supposed to be used. The gap was significant. People trusted outputs they didn't fully understand because there was nothing better.

The dashboard situation was the same story: ad-hoc requests, constant stress, no repeatable process. I ran interviews to understand what decisions people were actually trying to make.

What I built
Cleaned up the codebase: relative paths, proper date handling, updated model logic
Brought in new spatial data sources as better predictors of e-commerce demand than the originals
Ran PCA — the first validation the model had ever received — to build stakeholder confidence and expand adoption
Systematic dashboard process: SQL → Alteryx → Tableau, cut reporting time from 8 hours to 30 minutes
Set investment priorities for multi-year e-commerce facility network decisions
What changed

Stakeholders who had quietly doubted the outputs now had documented evidence they could trust. The ad-hoc dashboard chaos became a repeatable, user-centric process that the team actually wanted to use.

The real change: multi-million dollar facility decisions started being made on a model that had actually been validated. That's not a small thing.

Kitchen-table scale. Enterprise-level problem-solving.

My kids go through library books faster than I can place holds manually. 2,400+ books in the backlog. Two library accounts. A process that required navigating multiple systems, searching by title, switching accounts — every single book, every single week.

It was a broken process. So I automated it. Selenium navigates the Polaris library system, OCR pulls titles from screenshots, and the script handles dual-account management with automatic ILL fallback when a book isn't available locally. I haven't manually placed a library hold since.

I include this because it's the same instinct I bring to enterprise work — just at kitchen-table scale. I genuinely cannot walk past a broken process. The scale changes. The instinct doesn't.
