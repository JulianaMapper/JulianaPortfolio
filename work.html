PH360: Built for the people running local public health

AI platform for departments that can't afford enterprise tools — HIPAA-compliant, Sunshine Law-ready, built on AWS Bedrock.

The situation

Local public health departments are chronically under-resourced. They're handling disease surveillance, environmental health complaints, emergency response, and a hundred other functions — often with teams of five to twenty people. After COVID, many were asked to do even more with even less.

I'd been working in and around local health departments for years. I knew the workflows. I knew the pain. When I saw Claude's capabilities demonstrated at an AWS event, I didn't think "enterprise use case." I thought about the environmental health specialist in a small county spending half her day answering the same food code questions by phone. I thought about the emergency planner manually compiling reports that an AI could generate in minutes. The gap between what was possible and what these teams had access to was enormous — and nobody was building for them specifically.

The challenge wasn't technical appetite. It was that the tools that existed weren't built for public health's unique constraints: HIPAA compliance, Sunshine Law data retention requirements, limited IT staff, and leadership that needed policy cover before they could say yes to anything new.

What I heard

I personally found people, structured the interviews, and led 50+ conversations with public health professionals across the US. The real blocker wasn't technology — it was policy ambiguity. Agencies didn't know what was allowed. ("You can't use Google Docs but you can use a commercial AI tool" — genuinely the guidance some teams were getting.)

The second finding: even where AI was technically permitted, staff had no path to building a compliant knowledge base. Their information was locked in forms, case reports, food code manuals, and legacy systems — with no clear way to make it searchable or conversational without creating legal exposure.

A small initial engagement grew into a flagship partnership: strategic plan, AI implementation, 2024 mutual plenary. The AI Policy Guide became the resource health departments use to get leadership buy-in.

The situation

The U.S. military needed a unified tool for evaluating base locations worldwide — and had been making those decisions with fragmented data and gut instinct. There was no playbook. Nobody had built this before.

My mentor Jim Westervelt — creator of GRASS GIS — gave me the only advice that mattered on day one: go talk to the people who will use it before you build anything. I've done that ever since.

What I heard

I conducted user engagement across DOD to understand what data they trusted, what decisions they actually needed to make, and what "good enough" looked like in the field versus in a briefing room. The answer was different in every theater.

The presenting need was "a better map." The real need was a decision support system that could integrate dozens of conflicting data sources and give commanders something they could trust under pressure.

Puerto Rico — Hurricane Maria Response

Deployed as GIS Team Lead to Puerto Rico after Hurricane Maria. Built the geospatial platform supporting the Army Corps power restoration mission — reduced daily situation data updates from 8 hours to 30 minutes. Six months embedded with emergency response teams, coordinating geospatial data across federal and local agencies under active disaster conditions.

What changed

ENSITE deployed in Iraq, Afghanistan, and Poland after Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Reduced time to locate and establish military bases. Received the Department of the Army Medal for Civilian Service for the CB-SITE leadership component.

The data governance framework I built from scratch became the standard for how CERL managed geospatial data products. The publication record — journal articles, ERDC technical reports, conference presentations — built credibility for the entire program.

The situation

I inherited a geospatial demand forecasting model built by outside consultants. Variables were hard-coded. File paths were hard-coded. And — the thing that stopped me cold — nobody had ever validated it.

The company was making multi-year, multi-million dollar e-commerce facility network decisions — where to build warehouses, which stores to use for fulfillment — on a model nobody had stress-tested. I couldn't leave that alone.

What I heard

Before touching anything, I conducted user assessments and led focus groups to understand how the model was actually being used versus how it was supposed to be used. The gap was significant. People trusted outputs they didn't fully understand because there was nothing better.

The dashboard situation was the same story: ad-hoc requests, constant stress, no repeatable process. I ran interviews to understand what decisions people were actually trying to make.

What I built
Cleaned up the codebase: relative paths, proper date handling, updated model logic
Brought in new spatial data sources as better predictors of e-commerce demand than the originals
Ran PCA — the first validation the model had ever received — to build stakeholder confidence and expand adoption
Systematic dashboard process: SQL → Alteryx → Tableau, 1000% reduction in processing time
Set investment priorities for multi-year e-commerce facility network decisions
What changed

Expanded adoption of the demand model across the organization. Stakeholders who had quietly doubted the outputs now had documented evidence they could trust. The ad-hoc dashboard chaos became a repeatable, user-centric process that the team actually wanted to use.

The real change: multi-million dollar facility decisions started being made on a model that had actually been validated. That's not a small thing.

Personal · Automation · Always

A small problem. Automated.

Not every fix is enterprise-scale. This one is just how I think.

My kids go through library books faster than I can place holds manually. 2,400+ books in the backlog. Two library accounts. A process that required navigating multiple systems, searching by title, switching accounts — every single book, every single week.

It was a broken process. So I automated it. Selenium navigates the Polaris library system, OCR pulls titles from screenshots, and the script handles dual-account management with automatic ILL fallback when a book isn't available locally. I haven't manually placed a library hold since.

I include this because it's the same instinct I bring to enterprise work — just at kitchen-table scale. I genuinely cannot walk past a broken process. The scale changes. The instinct doesn't.
