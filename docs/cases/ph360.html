The diagnosis

Everyone assumed health departments needed better AI tools. I ran 50 interviews and found the actual problem: staff were copying protected health information into free consumer chatbots. Directors trusted outputs they hadn't validated. Legal teams didn't know the exposure. The technology wasn't the problem. The policy vacuum was.

Nobody had built a HIPAA-compliant AI platform specifically for local health departments. That gap was the product.

"Directors trusted AI outputs they hadn't validated. Staff used free consumer tools with PHI. Legal teams didn't know. The biggest thing I built before the product was a policy framework."
What the interviews actually found

The presenting problem was capacity. The real problem was policy. Every organization I talked to had the same gap: people were using AI tools they didn't have governance for, with data they shouldn't have been sharing, in ways nobody had officially sanctioned. Before a single line of code, I built the policy framework. That became its own deliverable.

Staff were using Gmail and free ChatGPT accounts with PHI. Directors trusted AI outputs for grant writing without understanding limitations. Legal and privacy teams didn't know the exposure. The most valuable thing I built before the product was an AI policy framework — which I now deliver as a training and workshop.

Model selection — the part nobody talks about

I tested 5+ LLMs against real public health tasks before choosing a stack: OpenRouter for API-swappable comparison, all available Gemini models, all Anthropic models. I ran five benchmarks per model — public health grant writing, policy summarization, data interpretation, response drafting, and a technical ArcPy task — and scored outputs blind.

AWS Bedrock won for one non-negotiable reason: HIPAA compliance. Bedrock keeps data within your AWS org instance, which makes it possible to sign a BAA. No other commercial LLM provider offered that for our use case at the time. Once I committed to Bedrock, I continued testing Claude Sonnet, Haiku, and Opus within that environment to find the right tradeoffs between cost and output quality.

"Five benchmarks. Five LLMs. Scored blind. Then one non-negotiable: HIPAA. The test results didn't matter if the data couldn't stay inside the customer's environment."
What I built

I hand-coded the first prototype in HTML — not to ship it, but to get something in front of real users fast. That prototype became the basis for our MVP feedback cycle. I used SAFe to structure the build, which let us move iteratively while keeping compliance documentation in sync.

HIPAA-compliant RAG architecture on AWS Bedrock — data stays within the customer's AWS org
Knowledge base builder — departments upload their own policies, data, and procedures
AI policy workshop and training — now a standalone offering
Multi-LLM testing framework — built and documented so future model updates are systematic, not ad hoc
Sunshine Law compliance layer — ensures AI-generated outputs can survive public records requests
What changed
"It's like having a really smart colleague who has read every policy we've ever written and never forgets anything."

— Sauk County Health Department

At Sauk County, routine tasks that took 30 minutes now take 5. That's not "AI made things faster" — that's staff getting back hours per week to do the work that actually requires a human.

The AI policy framework became its own deliverable. Health departments needed a policy before they could safely use any AI tool, and we're now the ones who help them build it.
